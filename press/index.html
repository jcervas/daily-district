Daily District turns the U.S. congressional map into a game you can play in two minutes

A free daily puzzle from a Carnegie Mellon redistricting scholar and the Redistrict Network challenges players to name all 435 U.S. House districts — starting with nothing but a shape on the map.

Pittsburgh, Pa., July 13, 2026 — Most Americans can picture their state. Almost none can picture their congressional district — the boundary that actually decides who represents them in the U.S. House. Daily District, a free browser game launching today at daily-district.com, sets out to change that, one shape at a time.

Each day, the game presents a single mystery district. Players first identify the state, then the exact district, working from a short stack of clues: the district's outline on the map, geographic and demographic hints, and hot-or-cold feedback as their guesses close in. Win or lose, every round ends with a full profile of that district — its current representative, Census demographics, recent election results, and geography — so a two-minute puzzle doubles as a civics lesson.

“The congressional district is one of the most consequential maps in American life, and one of the least understood. We wanted to make it something people actually want to learn and explore.”

Dr. Jonathan Cervas, Carnegie Mellon University · co-creator, Daily District

“Redistricting shapes American democracy, but it's almost always explained with lawsuits and acronyms. We wanted the opposite: something you can play over coffee and walk away knowing your map a little better than you did yesterday.”

Jay Fierman, Redistrict Network · co-creator, Daily District

The game is free, requires no download, and works on phones and desktops alike. A new puzzle unlocks each day; solved puzzles join a growing archive players can replay, and profiles of all 435 U.S. House districts are available to browse anytime. Play at daily-district.com and follow @daily_district_ on X for each day's puzzle.

About Daily District

Daily District is an educational game that challenges players to identify all 435 U.S. House districts through geographic, map-based, and contextual clues. Created through a partnership between Dr. Jonathan Cervas of Carnegie Mellon University and Jay Fierman of the Redistrict Network, it is an interactive way to learn about redistricting and how congressional districts shape American democracy — building civic knowledge for curious people of all backgrounds.
